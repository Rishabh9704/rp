Lightweight Architectures and Compression Methods for Efficient Computer Vision

Authors: Rishabh Anand, Anand Nishad, Vishwraj Singh, Aditiya Pratap Singh
Department of Computer Science, Institute of Engineering and Technology (IET), Agra

Abstract

The explosive growth of computer vision has been fueled primarily by increasingly sophisticated deep neural networks. However, the computational and memory requirements of these models often make them unsuitable for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices. This research presents a comprehensive analysis of lightweight architectures and compression methods that enable efficient computer vision systems without significantly compromising accuracy. We explore three main approaches: designing inherently efficient architectures, compressing existing models, and hardware-aware optimization. Our findings demonstrate that combining these techniques can reduce model size by up to 90% while maintaining competitive performance. This work provides a roadmap for building robust but realistic computer vision systems that can be large-scale-deployed for the next generation of applications.

1.0 Introduction: The Need for Efficient Vision Systems

Computer vision has emerged as a cornerstone of modern AI, powering applications from autonomous vehicles to smart city infrastructure. However, the gap between state-of-the-art model performance and practical deployment constraints continues to widen. Traditional deep learning models require substantial computational resources, making them impractical for edge deployment. This has led to two prevailing directions: architecting lightweight-by-design models and compressing classic models.

2.0 Challenges in Efficient Computer Vision

2.1 Hardware and Resource Constraints

Edge devices such as smartphones, drones, and industrial sensors have constrained computational capabilities, memory, and battery life. These limitations necessitate careful consideration of model complexity, inference speed, and energy consumption. The challenge extends beyond individual device constraints to encompass the entire deployment ecosystem, highlighting the need for co-design of hardware, software, and algorithms.

2.3 Privacy, Security, and Ethical Issues

Extensive use of computer vision systems raises ethical and privacy concerns, including unauthorized surveillance and biometric data collection. Edge deployment introduces additional security challenges, as distributed IoT devices are often vulnerable to malware and network threats. Ensuring data privacy while maintaining system functionality requires careful architectural considerations.

6.0 Conclusion

The shift from cloud-centric to edge-centric vision is a strategic move toward efficient, secure, and privacy-sensitive AI deployment. By combining lightweight architectures, compression techniques, and hardware-aware design, we can achieve significant reductions in computational requirements while maintaining acceptable accuracy. This approach enables next-generation autonomous and real-time applications across diverse domains.
